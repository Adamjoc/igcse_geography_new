Topic 1 · Changing River Environments

1.1–1.3▼

1.1.1 Drainage Basin Systems

A drainage basin is the area of land drained by a river and its tributaries — a system with inputs, processes, stores and outputs.

  • Watershed — ridge of high land forming the boundary between drainage basins
  • Tributary — smaller river joining the main channel; Confluence — where two rivers meet
  • Discharge — volume of water passing a point per second (m³/s = cumecs)

A flood hydrograph shows discharge over time after a storm. A steep rising limb and short lag time indicate a flashy basin. Causes: impermeable rock, steep slopes, little vegetation, urban surfaces, saturated soils.

1.1.2 The Bradshaw Model NEW 2027

Describes how river characteristics change downstream as discharge increases:

VariableChange downstreamReason
Discharge▲ IncreasesMore tributaries add water
Velocity▲ IncreasesSmoother channel = less friction
Channel width▲ IncreasesLateral erosion widens channel
Channel depth▲ IncreasesErosion and deposition deepen channel
Load quantity▲ IncreasesMore tributaries add sediment
Load particle size▼ DecreasesAttrition wears particles smaller
Bed roughness▼ DecreasesSmaller, rounder particles smoother
Gradient (slope)▼ DecreasesRiver approaches base level (sea)

Exam tip: link changes between variables in your answers — the model is a simplification.

1.2 River Processes

Erosion — 4 types

  • Hydraulic action — force of water compresses air in cracks, widening them
  • Abrasion — sediment scrapes and sandpapers the channel bed and banks
  • Attrition — particles collide, becoming smaller and rounder downstream
  • Solution — slightly acidic water dissolves soluble rock (limestone, chalk)

Transportation — 4 types

  • Traction — large boulders rolled along the bed
  • Saltation — pebbles bounce along the bed in a leaping motion
  • Suspension — fine silt and clay carried within the flow (gives rivers brown colour)
  • Solution — dissolved minerals transported invisibly in the water

Deposition

Occurs when velocity falls and the river loses energy. Heaviest particles deposited first. Triggers: reduced gradient, increased friction, reduced discharge, flooding onto a floodplain.

1.3 River Landforms

Upper course

  • V-shaped valley — dominant vertical erosion cuts downwards; steep sides, narrow floor
  • Interlocking spurs — river winds around resistant rock ridges that interlock like teeth
  • Waterfalls & gorges — hard cap rock over softer rock; softer rock undercut → cap collapses → waterfall retreats upstream leaving a gorge
Case Study · Upper Course Landforms

River Tees & High Force Waterfall, NE England

  • Drop of 21 metres — one of England's largest waterfalls
  • Hard Whin Sill dolerite cap rock overlies softer limestone and shale
  • Hydraulic action and abrasion undercut softer rock → plunge pool → cap collapses → waterfall retreats upstream leaving a gorge ~500m long
  • Classic V-shaped valley and interlocking spurs visible upstream of High Force
[PHOTO: High Force waterfall — dolerite cap rock, plunge pool, and gorge; geological layers clearly visible]

Middle course

  • Meanders — fast flow on outer bank erodes (river cliff / bluff); slow flow on inner bank deposits (slip-off slope / point bar)
  • Oxbow lakes — meander neck narrows → river cuts through during flood → meander abandoned and sealed, forming a crescent lake
[PHOTO: Aerial view of meandering river showing river cliffs, point bars, and an oxbow lake in the floodplain]

Lower course

  • Floodplain — flat land either side of the channel; built up by alluvium during floods; fertile soils
  • Levées — natural embankments of coarse sediment beside the channel; coarsest particles drop first as velocity falls
  • Delta — high sediment load meets low-energy coast; sediment deposited faster than removed, building a fan of land
Case Study · Delta Opportunities 1.3.1

Nile Delta, Egypt

  • Extends 160 km into the Mediterranean, covering ~22,000 km²
  • Agriculture: extraordinarily fertile alluvial soils; cotton, rice, vegetables; supports ~40 million people
  • Settlement & trade: Egypt's most densely populated region; proximity to Alexandria and the Suez Canal
  • Threat: Aswan High Dam (1970) traps sediment upstream → delta no longer replenished → coastal erosion up to 100m/year in places; rising sea levels accelerate this
[PHOTO: Satellite image of Nile Delta — fan shape, green agricultural land against desert, Mediterranean coastline]

1.3.5–1.3.7 River Pollution NEW 2027

Causes

  • Agricultural runoff — fertilisers (nitrates/phosphates) cause eutrophication (algal blooms deplete oxygen, killing aquatic life); pesticides toxic to organisms
  • Industrial effluent — heavy metals (lead, cadmium, mercury), chemicals, dyes, thermal pollution
  • Sewage — untreated domestic waste introduces bacteria and pathogens; major issue in rapidly urbanising LIC cities
  • Mining — acid drainage, heavy metals leach into rivers
  • Urban runoff — oil, metals from roads washed in during rain events

Impacts

  • Ecological: biodiversity loss, fish kills, food chain collapse
  • Health: drinking water contamination (cholera, typhoid, heavy metal poisoning); elevated cancer rates near industrial polluters
  • Economic: fishing industry collapse, tourism decline, costly water treatment
  • Social: disproportionate impact on poorer communities living closest to polluting industries

Management

  • Environmental legislation and the Polluter Pays Principle
  • Wastewater treatment plants; riparian buffer zones (vegetation strips intercept agricultural runoff)
  • International agreements for cross-border rivers; water quality monitoring and enforcement

1.3.8 River Flooding — Bangladesh

Case Study · River Flooding

Bangladesh — Ganges–Brahmaputra Delta

Background: ~80% of Bangladesh is floodplain, less than 1m above sea level. Three rivers — the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna — converge here, draining a vast Himalayan catchment.

Physical causes

  • Heavy monsoon rainfall (June–September): ~80% of Bangladesh's 2,000mm annual rainfall falls in this period
  • Himalayan snowmelt adds to discharge in summer; flat deltaic topography — water has nowhere to drain
  • Bay of Bengal storm surges push seawater inland

Human causes

  • Deforestation in Nepal and northern India increases runoff reaching Bangladesh
  • Population pressure forces settlement on flood-prone chars (river islands)
  • Inadequate urban drainage; climate change intensifying monsoons and sea level rise

Impacts

  • Social: millions displaced annually; homes destroyed; deaths from drowning and waterborne disease; disruption to education
  • Economic: rice and jute crops destroyed; transport links cut; Bangladesh loses estimated 1–2% of GDP annually to flooding

Responses

  • Flood Action Plan: embankments (flood bunds) along major rivers — effective but can increase intensity elsewhere if breached
  • Early warning systems: satellite monitoring gives several days' notice; community radio reaches rural areas
  • Flood-resistant housing: raised plinths, flood shelters, raised evacuation roads; World Bank and NGO support

1.3.9 Pollution Case Study — Pearl River Delta NEW 2027

Case Study · River Pollution

Pearl River Delta (PRD), Southern China

Context: Encompasses Guangzhou, Shenzhen, Dongguan, and Hong Kong — a megalopolis of ~75 million people. China's Special Economic Zones (SEZs) policy from 1979 prioritised rapid industrial growth, making the PRD the "world's factory".

Causes

  • Industrial effluent: electronics, textiles, plastics, metal processing discharged heavy metals (lead, cadmium), dyes, chemicals directly into rivers
  • E-waste — Guiyu: the "world's e-waste capital"; informal dismantling of discarded electronics; burning and acid-washing released toxic lead, mercury, dioxins
  • Rapid urbanisation: inadequate sewage infrastructure; untreated waste into waterways
  • Air pollution: coal power and factory emissions; PM2.5 regularly 10× WHO safe limits

Impacts

  • Water: by 2005, 80% of Pearl River tributaries rated unfit for human use; fish stocks collapsed; drinking water for millions compromised
  • Health: "Cancer villages" — elevated cancer rates, respiratory disease, birth defects along contaminated tributaries; in Guiyu, children's blood lead levels 6× acceptable limits
  • Ecological: aquatic biodiversity loss; contaminated farmland soils

Responses

  • Pearl River Clean-Up: wastewater treatment plants; water quality in main channel improved significantly by 2020
  • Air quality: coal replaced by gas/renewables; vehicle standards tightened; PM2.5 fell ~35% between 2013 and 2020
  • Factory relocation: most polluting industries moved inland or overseas; PRD shifting to high-tech and services
  • Guiyu: formal regulated e-waste recycling park opened 2014; informal dismantling significantly reduced
  • Legislation: environmental courts; Polluter Pays enforcement; environmental targets in officials' performance assessments

Topic 2 · Changing Coastal Environments

2.1–2.3▼

2.1 Coastal Processes

Fetch = distance of open water over which wind blows; greater fetch = more powerful waves.

  • Constructive waves — low frequency (6–8/min), strong swash, weak backwash → deposition → builds beaches
  • Destructive waves — high frequency (10–14/min), weak swash, strong backwash → erosion → cliff retreat

Erosion — same 4 processes as rivers

  • Hydraulic action — wave impact compresses air in cracks (most powerful in storms)
  • Abrasion — waves hurl sediment against the cliff face, undercutting it
  • Attrition — particles wear each other down during transport
  • Solution — seawater dissolves limestone and chalk

Longshore drift (LSD)

Waves approach beach at an angle; swash carries sediment up at the wave angle; backwash returns it straight down (gravity). Net movement is along the coast. Controls formation of spits, bars, and tombolos.

2.2 Coastal Landforms

Erosional

  • Wave-cut notch & platform — waves attack cliff at waterline → notch → cliff collapse → retreating platform of rock at wave level
  • Caves → arches → stacks → stumps — hydraulic action exploits weaknesses in headlands; progressive collapse sequence
  • Bays and headlands — alternating hard/soft rock; soft rock erodes into bays, hard rock resists as headlands

Depositional

  • Beach — sediment accumulates in sheltered zone between tides
  • Spit — LSD extends beach into open water; curves where wind direction changes; salt marsh forms behind
  • Bar — spit extends across a bay, trapping a lagoon; Tombolo — spit connects mainland to offshore island

2.3 Coastal Management

Hard engineering

  • Sea walls — reflect wave energy; expensive but effective; can cause erosion downdrift
  • Groynes — interrupt LSD, build up beach; cause erosion beyond the groyne field
  • Rock armour (rip-rap) — boulders absorb wave energy; cheaper; visually impactful

Soft engineering

  • Beach nourishment — adding imported sand/shingle; needs regular replacement; natural appearance
  • Dune stabilisation — marram grass planting; low cost; ecological benefit
  • Managed retreat — allowing coastline to move inland; cheapest option; politically difficult
Case Study · Coastal Erosional Landforms

Ponta da Piedade, Algarve, Portugal

  • Jurassic limestone and sandstone headland near Lagos, southern Algarve
  • Differential erosion has created caves, arches, stacks, and grottos — a textbook erosional sequence
  • Harder layers form resistant arches; collapsed arches form isolated stacks; wave-cut notches visible at cliff bases
  • Atlantic swell waves drive hydraulic action and abrasion; tourist pressure adds footpath erosion as a management challenge
[PHOTO: Ponta da Piedade — golden limestone arches, sea stacks, and grottos showing the full erosional sequence]
Case Study · Coastal Management

Costa da Caparica, Portugal

  • 25 km sandy Atlantic beach south of Lisbon; popular tourism and residential development in former dune system
  • Problem: storm erosion removes up to 3–4 metres of beach/year; urban development exposes infrastructure; rising sea levels increase storm risk
  • Hard management: groynes interrupt LSD; sea walls on developed sections; periodic beach nourishment with dredged offshore sand
  • Soft management: dune restoration; marram grass planting; pedestrian boardwalks to protect dune vegetation
  • Evaluation: groynes maintain beach width locally but cause downdrift erosion; nourishment expensive and temporary; managed retreat politically difficult given tourism economy
[PHOTO: Costa da Caparica beach showing groynes extending into the Atlantic, beach settlement, and dunes behind]

Topic 3 · Ecosystems & Environments

3.1–3.3▼

3.1 Ecosystem Fundamentals

An ecosystem is a community of living organisms interacting with each other and their non-living environment (soil, climate, water). Energy flows via food chains; nutrients are cycled.

  • Producer — plants that photosynthesise; Consumer — animals that eat producers or other consumers
  • Decomposer — bacteria/fungi breaking down dead matter, returning nutrients to soil
  • Nutrient cycle — nutrients move between biomass, litter, and soil stores; Biodiversity — variety of species; higher biodiversity = more resilient ecosystem

3.2 Tropical Rainforest (TRF)

Characteristics

  • Located between Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn; South America, Central Africa, SE Asia
  • Climate: high temperature (~27°C year-round), high rainfall (>2000mm/yr), no dry season
  • Layered structure: emergent layer → canopy → understorey → shrub layer → forest floor
  • Nutrient cycle: rapid decomposition in warm/wet conditions; most nutrients locked in biomass, not soil — soil is actually nutrient-poor (latosol); clearing forest removes nutrients permanently
  • Adaptations: buttress roots for stability in shallow soil; drip-tip leaves to shed water; lianas, epiphytes, bromeliads
Case Study · Tropical Rainforest

Amazon Rainforest, South America

  • World's largest TRF: 5.5 million km², spanning 9 countries (60% Brazil)
  • Contains ~40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, 3,000 fish species
  • Deforestation drivers: cattle ranching (~70% of clearance), soya farming, logging (legal and illegal), road building opening new areas, mining
  • Rate: Brazil lost 11,568 km² in 2022; down from peak years but still severe
  • Impacts: carbon release (~100bn tonnes stored); biodiversity loss; disruption to water cycle (trees recycle rainfall — less forest = less regional rainfall); displacement of indigenous peoples
  • Management: REDD+ scheme (payments for avoided deforestation); 30% of Brazilian Amazon legally protected; Lula government (2023) reversed deforestation — fell 50% in 2023; Fairtrade soya certification pressuring supply chains
[PHOTO: Aerial view of Amazon deforestation — sharp boundary between dense green rainforest and cleared cattle pasture]

3.3 Antarctica — Cold Environment

Characteristics

  • Earth's coldest, windiest, driest continent; average temperatures −50°C inland, −10°C coast
  • Ice sheet averaging 2.3 km thick — holds 60% of world's fresh water
  • Food web: phytoplankton → krill (keystone species) → fish → seals → penguins/whales; remove krill and the whole food web collapses
Case Study · Cold Environment

Antarctica

Value

  • Scientific: ice cores reveal 800,000 years of climate data; ozone hole first discovered here; global climate monitoring base
  • Tourism: ~80,000 visitors/year; ecotourism revenue, but risks include ship fuel, waste, and non-native species introduction
  • Resources: estimated 36–160 billion barrels oil beneath the seabed; vast mineral deposits — currently protected until 2048

Threats

  • Climate change: West Antarctic Ice Sheet losing mass; Thwaites Glacier could raise sea levels 65 cm if fully lost
  • Krill decline: warming seas and sea ice loss threaten krill → collapse of food web
  • Pollution: microplastics detected in Antarctic snow; ozone hole (now recovering) increased UV exposure

Management

  • Antarctic Treaty (1959): 54 nations; demilitarised, scientific cooperation; no territorial claims activated
  • Protocol on Environmental Protection (1991): bans mining until 2048; strict waste management rules
  • IAATO: limits tourist ship sizes and shore visits; self-regulating industry body
[PHOTO: Antarctic ice shelf or glacier — vast blue/white expanse; ideally with research ship or penguins for scale]

Topic 4 · Hazardous Environments

4.1–4.3▼

4.1 Plate Tectonics

  • Convergent (destructive) — denser oceanic plate subducts under continental → explosive volcanoes, earthquakes, fold mountains (e.g. Andes, Japan)
  • Divergent (constructive) — plates move apart → magma rises → shield volcanoes, rift valleys (e.g. Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East African Rift)
  • Conservative — plates slide past each other → earthquakes only, no volcanoes (e.g. San Andreas Fault)

Earthquakes

Stress builds at plate boundaries → sudden release → seismic waves from the focus (depth below surface) and epicentre (point on surface directly above). Measured on Moment Magnitude Scale (logarithmic — each point = 10× more energy). Shallow focus earthquakes cause more surface damage.

Volcanoes

  • Shield volcanoes — gentle slopes, runny basaltic lava, less explosive (divergent/hotspot, e.g. Hawaii)
  • Composite/strato volcanoes — steep sides, viscous lava, explosive eruptions (convergent). Most deadly hazards: pyroclastic flows (700°C gas+ash at 100+ km/h), lahars (volcanic mudflows), tephra/ash fall

4.2 Tectonic Case Studies

Case Study · Volcanic Eruption — LIC

Nevado del Ruiz / Armero, Colombia — 13 November 1985

  • Composite stratovolcano at 5,321m; Nazca plate subducting under South American plate
  • Moderate eruption (VEI 3) melted the Ruiz ice cap → massive lahars channelled down river valleys at 50 km/h
  • Town of Armero (pop. 29,000) destroyed in minutes; ~23,000 deaths
  • Why so deadly: no effective evacuation warning issued; residents told to stay put; night-time eruption; poverty limited evacuation capacity; a hazard map showing Armero at risk had been produced weeks before — the tragedy was preventable
  • Armero was never rebuilt; site remains a memorial
[PHOTO: Armero after the lahar — ruins partially buried in grey volcanic mud, scale of destruction visible]
Case Study · Earthquake + Tsunami — NEE

Tōhoku Earthquake & Tsunami, Japan — 11 March 2011

  • Magnitude 9.1 megathrust; Japan Trench; Pacific plate subducting under North American plate; focus ~32 km deep
  • 40-metre tsunami waves reached the coast within 30–40 minutes
  • 18,500 people killed total; 400,000 buildings destroyed or damaged
  • Fukushima Daiichi: tsunami disabled cooling systems → partial nuclear meltdown → radioactive contamination → 150,000 evacuated from 20 km exclusion zone
  • Cost: estimated US$360 billion — most expensive natural disaster in history
  • Response: 100,000 Self-Defense Force troops; US military assistance (Operation Tomodachi); ¥25 trillion reconstruction programme; 400 km of new seawall (up to 15m high)
  • Why better prepared than Armero? P-wave earthquake early warning, tsunami warning buoys, evacuation routes, earthquake-resistant buildings, regular public drills — yet scale exceeded design specifications
[PHOTO: Tōhoku coastal town after tsunami — buildings destroyed, large debris field, scale of destruction clearly visible]
Case Study · Volcanic Threat — HIC

Mount Vesuvius, Italy — Threat to Naples

  • Bay of Naples; Eurasian–African convergent boundary; last erupted 1944; Europe's most dangerous active volcano
  • 79 CE eruption: buried Pompeii and Herculaneum in pyroclastic surges; ~16,000 killed; UNESCO World Heritage Site
  • Current threat: ~600,000 people live in the Red Zone; 3 million in greater Naples; magma chamber monitoring shows ongoing activity; evacuation time needed: minimum 72 hours
  • Management: National Emergency Plan — Red/Yellow/Blue risk zones; evacuation routes planned; €30,000 voluntary relocation incentive for Red Zone residents (largely undersubscribed)
  • Monitoring: INGV continuously monitors ground deformation, seismicity, and gas emissions
  • Challenge: population has actually grown in the Red Zone since 1944 — familiarity breeds complacency; tourism economy incentivises staying
[PHOTO: Mount Vesuvius viewed from Naples or Bay of Naples — dense urban population in foreground, volcanic cone behind]

Topic 5 · Weather, Climate & Change

5.1–5.3▼

5.1 Weather and Climate

Weather = short-term atmospheric conditions at a place. Climate = average weather patterns over 30+ years. Climate zones are determined by latitude, altitude, distance from sea, ocean currents, and prevailing winds.

5.2 Climate Change — Evidence and Causes

Evidence

  • Global average temperature risen ~1.2°C since pre-industrial levels (IPCC 2023)
  • Sea level rise: 20 cm since 1900; currently accelerating at 3.6mm/yr
  • Arctic sea ice extent declining; glaciers retreating globally; more frequent extreme weather events
  • CO₂ now at 420 ppm — highest in 3 million years

Causes

  • Enhanced greenhouse effect: CO₂, methane (CH₄), nitrous oxide (N₂O) trap outgoing heat. Human sources: burning fossil fuels, deforestation, agriculture (livestock, rice paddies), cement production, landfill
  • Natural causes (Milankovitch orbital cycles, solar variation, volcanic eruptions) cannot explain the current rapid rate of warming
Case Study · Climate Change Impact — SIDS

The Maldives, Indian Ocean

  • 1,192 coral islands; average elevation: 1.5m above sea level; population ~540,000; economy dependent on tourism (28%) and fishing
  • Sea level rise threat: projections suggest 80% of islands uninhabitable by 2050 (high emissions scenario); storm surges already flood land; saltwater intrusion contaminates freshwater lens
  • Coral bleaching: warming seas bleach reefs → loss of natural wave protection → island erosion; fishing and tourism both threatened
  • Adaptation: Hulhumalé — artificial island built 2m above sea level, planned to house 240,000 people; Malé has a 3m sea wall; stilted buildings; exploring purchasing land in Sri Lanka/India/Australia
  • Mitigation: committed to carbon neutrality by 2030; 100% renewable energy target; high-profile international advocacy at COP summits
  • Climate justice: Maldives contributes <0.003% of global emissions yet faces existential threat — a key argument for differentiated responsibility
[PHOTO: Maldives island — low-lying land, turquoise lagoon, coral reef, settlements barely above sea level]
[PHOTO: Malé sea wall or Hulhumalé artificial island construction showing adaptation infrastructure]

5.3 Responses to Climate Change — COP26

  • Mitigation — reducing emissions to slow climate change: renewable energy, electric vehicles, reforestation, carbon capture, reduced deforestation
  • Adaptation — adjusting to impacts already happening: sea walls, drought-resistant crops, flood early warning systems, managed retreat
Case Study · International Climate Response

COP26 — Glasgow, November 2021

  • What is COP? Conference of the Parties — annual UN climate summit where 197 countries negotiate action under the UNFCCC
  • Glasgow Climate Pact: first COP agreement to explicitly call for "phase down" of unabated coal (India and China blocked "phase out"); 1.5°C target reaffirmed; NDCs to be strengthened annually
  • Methane pledge: 100+ countries pledged to reduce methane by 30% by 2030 (from 2020 levels)
  • Deforestation pledge: 140 countries (90% of world's forests) pledged to end deforestation by 2030
  • Finance: $100bn/yr climate finance for developing countries — promised since 2009, still not fully delivered at time of COP26
  • Evaluation: praised for keeping 1.5°C alive; criticised for insufficient ambition, lack of binding commitments, and inadequate adaptation finance for LICs

Topic 6 · Population

6.1–6.3▼

6.1 Population Change

Natural increase = Birth Rate − Death Rate. The Demographic Transition Model (DTM) shows how birth and death rates change as countries develop:

  • Stage 1: High BR, High DR → fluctuating low population (pre-industrial; no countries remain here)
  • Stage 2: High BR, Falling DR → rapid growth (many LICs — improved medicine, sanitation, food)
  • Stage 3: Falling BR, Low DR → slowing growth (NEEs — urbanisation, education, women's rights)
  • Stage 4: Low BR, Low DR → stable/slow growth (most HICs)
  • Stage 5: BR below DR → natural decrease (Japan, Germany, some S. European countries)

Population pyramids: wide base = youthful/high BR (LIC/Stage 2); top-heavy = ageing (HIC/Stage 4–5). Pyramid shape reflects DTM stage.

Factors affecting birth rate

Education of women (especially secondary+); contraception access; female employment; infant mortality rates; religious/cultural norms; government policy; urbanisation (children less economically valuable in cities).

6.2 Population Policies

Case Study · Anti-Natalist Policy

China — One-Child Policy (1979–2015)

  • Policy: most urban couples limited to one child; rural families could have two if first was a girl; ethnic minorities exempt; enforced by fines, financial incentives, and in some areas coercive sterilisation/abortion
  • Results: fertility rate fell from ~5.8 (1970) to ~1.5 (2000); estimated 400 million fewer births; demographic dividend accelerated economic growth (large working-age population, small dependants)
  • Problems: severe gender imbalance (male:female 1.18:1 by 2010 due to sex-selective abortion/infanticide); 4-2-1 problem (one adult child supporting two parents and four grandparents); ageing population crisis; human rights violations
  • Now: two-child (2015) → three-child (2021) → all limits removed (2023). Birth rate remains stubbornly low — urbanisation, cost of living, and cultural shift persist despite policy reversal
[PHOTO: One-Child Policy propaganda poster or family planning billboard in China — archival/historical context]
Case Study · Pro-Natalist Policy

France

  • Tools: generous family allowances per child from the second; free crèche from age 2; 16 weeks full-pay maternity leave; 28 days paternity leave; tax incentives (quotient familial) rewarding larger families
  • Result: France has one of Europe's highest fertility rates at ~1.83 (2022) — well above EU average ~1.46, though still below replacement (2.1)
  • Evaluation: shows targeted financial and childcare support can maintain relatively higher birth rates; but cannot reach replacement level without immigration; cost ~3% of GDP on family policy
Case Study · Youthful Population

Uganda

  • Total Fertility Rate ~4.7; median age 15.7 years; population 48 million (2024), projected 130 million by 2050
  • Causes of high growth: high cultural value of large families; limited female education; poor contraception access; high infant mortality encouraging more births; rural agricultural economy (children as labour)
  • Challenges: school system overwhelmed; high youth unemployment; land fragmentation; food insecurity; stretched government services
  • Potential: a demographic dividend is possible if young people can be educated and employed — large working-age population could drive growth as it did in East Asia
[PHOTO: Crowded Ugandan primary school classroom illustrating pressure of youthful population on education services]
Case Study · Ageing Population

Japan

  • Fertility rate ~1.2 (2023) — lowest ever recorded; 29% of population aged 65+; population declining since 2008
  • Causes: high cost of raising children in cities; long working hours culture; women prioritising careers; housing costs; cultural norms requiring marriage before children
  • Challenges: pension burden on shrinking workforce; labour shortages in manufacturing, nursing, construction; rural depopulation and "ghost villages"; national debt (240% of GDP) partly from escalating welfare costs
  • Responses: baby bonus schemes; extended childcare; encouraging female workforce participation; robotics and automation; cautious increase in skilled worker immigration (cultural resistance remains strong)
  • Evaluation: even significant policy intervention cannot quickly reverse low fertility entrenched in a highly urbanised, high-cost economy
[PHOTO: Ageing Japan — elderly workers, or a depopulated rural Japanese village showing decline]

6.3 Migration

Push factors drive people away (poverty, conflict, natural disaster, lack of opportunity). Pull factors attract migrants (employment, safety, better services, family connections).

  • Benefits for host: fills labour shortages, demographic rejuvenation, tax contributions, skills, cultural diversity
  • Costs for host: pressure on housing and services, political tensions, integration challenges
  • Benefits for source: remittances, reduced population pressure
  • Costs for source: brain drain (loss of skilled workers), ageing rural populations, family disruption

Topic 7 · Urbanisation & Settlement

7.1–7.3▼

7.1 Urbanisation

Urbanisation = increasing proportion of the population living in urban areas. In 2023, 57% of the world is urban; projected 68% by 2050. Fastest growth in Asia and Africa.

Causes

  • Rural–urban migration — push (poverty, limited services, farm mechanisation, conflict) and pull (employment, education, healthcare, higher wages)
  • Natural increase — migrants tend to be young adults; birth rates in cities often exceed death rates

Issues in rapidly urbanising cities

  • Informal settlements (squatter settlements/slums) — housing built illegally on unoccupied or hazardous land; overcrowding; poor sanitation; insecure land tenure; limited clean water, electricity, healthcare, education
  • Traffic congestion, air pollution, waste management challenges, lack of green space

7.2 Urban Case Studies

Case Study · Informal Settlement

Dharavi, Mumbai, India

  • Central Mumbai; one of Asia's largest informal settlements; ~1 million people in 2.1 km² — one of the world's highest population densities
  • Economy: thriving informal economy; estimated annual turnover US$700m–1 billion; ~15,000 workshops; leather goods, pottery, recycling, textiles; 80% of India's leather exports processed here
  • Challenges: only one toilet per 1,440 people; open drains; clean water supply only 1–2 hours/day; cramped dwellings (<10m²); insecure tenure; fire risk; COVID-19 spread rapidly due to density
  • Improvements: Slum Rehabilitation Authority (SRA) schemes rehouse some residents while developers build on freed land; NGO health clinics; government road upgrades
  • 2022 redevelopment: Adani Group scheme plans tower block rehousing for all residents; controversial — residents risk losing community and livelihoods; those with <10 years' tenure may be excluded
[PHOTO: Dharavi aerial view — dense patchwork of tin-roofed dwellings and narrow alleyways, Mumbai skyscrapers visible in background]
Case Study · Sustainable Urban Development

Curitiba, Brazil

  • Capital of Paraná state, southern Brazil; ~1.97 million people; radical planning led by architect-mayor Jaime Lerner from 1971; became a global model for sustainable city planning
  • Bus Rapid Transit (BRT): integrated tube-station bus system with dedicated lanes; 2.3 million passengers/day; 70% of commuters use public transport; per capita fuel use 30% below Brazil's 8 largest cities
  • Green space: expanded from 0.5 m² to 52 m² per person since 1970; 16 parks, 14 forests; floodplain parks absorb excess water naturally, preventing flood events
  • Waste management: Green Exchange Programme — favela residents bring recyclable waste and receive bus tokens, food vouchers, or school supplies; recycling rate 70%; informal recyclers integrated into formal waste economy
  • Evaluation: shows urban planning can achieve environmental and social goals simultaneously; required consistent political will over decades; growing car ownership and population creating new sustainability pressures

Topic 8 · Development

8.1–8.3▼

8.1 Measuring Development

Economic indicators

  • GDP per capita — total economic output ÷ population; widely used but masks inequality within countries
  • GNI per capita — includes income earned abroad; more accurate for countries with many overseas workers

Social indicators

  • Life expectancy — reflects healthcare quality, sanitation, diet
  • Infant mortality rate (IMR) — deaths per 1,000 live births under age 1; highly sensitive indicator of development level
  • Literacy rate — % of adults who can read and write; key for economic participation

Composite indicator

Human Development Index (HDI) — combines GNI per capita, life expectancy, and education (mean/expected years of schooling) into a score of 0–1. More nuanced than GDP alone; published annually by UNDP.

Limitations

GDP per capita masks inequality (Gini coefficient measures this separately). HDI ignores gender inequality and environmental sustainability. No single indicator captures the full picture.

8.2 Causes of Uneven Development

  • Physical: landlocked countries face higher trade costs; tropical disease burden; natural disasters destroy infrastructure; poor soils limit agriculture
  • Historical: colonialism extracted resources; legacy of arbitrary borders and divided ethnic groups; dependency on raw material exports
  • Trade: HIC tariffs and subsidies disadvantage LIC farmers; unfair terms of trade
  • Debt: IMF/World Bank structural adjustment loans often imposed damaging conditions (privatisation, public spending cuts)
  • Governance: corruption diverts resources from public benefit; weak institutions

8.3 Development Strategies

Case Study · Development — LIC

Ethiopia

  • Population ~130 million; HDI rank 175/193 (2022); GDP per capita ~$1,020; 33% below poverty line
  • ADLI strategy: Agricultural Development Led Industrialisation — improve smallholder farming first, use surplus to fund industrialisation
  • Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD): 6,450 MW on the Blue Nile; Africa's largest dam; provides electricity for industry and enables export to neighbours; controversial with Egypt over downstream water security
  • Industrial parks: Hawassa Industrial Park (2016) — export-oriented garment manufacturing; 25,000 jobs; attracts TNCs; modelled on East Asian export zone model
  • Progress: GDP growth averaged 10%/year 2004–2019; child mortality fell from 123 to 45 per 1,000; near-universal primary school enrolment
  • Challenges: Tigray conflict (2020–22) reversed development gains; drought and food insecurity; rapid population growth; landlocked country with high transport costs
[PHOTO: Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) — construction or completed structure on the Blue Nile, showing scale]
Case Study · Development — NEE

China

  • Lifted 800 million people out of extreme poverty between 1978 and 2020 — the fastest large-scale poverty reduction in history
  • Strategy: export-led industrialisation; Special Economic Zones (SEZs) from 1979 — Shenzhen was first; foreign investment, free trade, flexible labour laws; rural migrants fuelled factory output
  • Government role: heavy state investment in infrastructure (high-speed rail, ports, highways); education expansion; Belt and Road Initiative (from 2013) — infrastructure investment in 150+ countries, expanding trade networks and political influence
  • Development indicators: HDI rank 79 (2022) vs 105 in 2000; life expectancy 67→78; literacy ~97%; GDP per capita $300→$13,000 (1980–2023)
  • Challenges: widening rural–urban inequality; environmental degradation (improving but severe legacy); ageing population; trade tensions with USA

Topic 9 · Economic Activity

9.1–9.3▼

9.1 Sectors of Economic Activity

  • Primary — raw materials from nature: farming, fishing, mining, forestry
  • Secondary — processing raw materials into goods: manufacturing, construction
  • Tertiary — services: retail, education, healthcare, transport, finance
  • Quaternary — knowledge economy: research, ICT, media, design

As countries develop, employment shifts primary → secondary → tertiary/quaternary (Clark-Fisher model). TNCs (transnational corporations) locate production in LICs/NEEs to exploit cheaper labour, lower environmental standards, and tax incentives, while keeping headquarters and R&D in HICs.

9.3 Economic Activity Case Studies

Case Study · TNC — Secondary Sector

Foxconn, China

  • Taiwanese-owned TNC; world's largest electronics manufacturer; >1 million workers in China; manufactures iPhones, iPads, PlayStations for Apple, Samsung, Sony
  • Locations: Zhengzhou (Henan), Shenzhen, Chengdu — selected for vast low-cost labour pool, government incentives (tax breaks, subsidised land), and export transport links
  • Benefits for China: millions of manufacturing jobs; technology transfer; tax revenue; development of inland provinces (Zhengzhou = "iPhone City")
  • Criticisms: wages low (~$300–400/month); 12-hour shifts; strict discipline; series of worker suicides at Shenzhen plant (2010) led to global scrutiny; anti-suicide nets installed — widely condemned as symptom-management; most profits repatriated to Taiwan (Foxconn) and USA (Apple)
  • Foxconn's view: workers earn above regional average; dormitory, canteen, and healthcare provided on-site
  • Change: post-COVID diversification to India (Tata partnership), Vietnam, and Mexico — reducing China dependence and demonstrating TNC footlooseness
[PHOTO: Foxconn factory campus in China — large industrial facility, worker dormitories, or electronics assembly line]
Case Study · Primary Sector — Commercial Farming

Kenya Horticulture Export Industry

  • Kenya is one of Africa's leading exporters of cut flowers, vegetables, and fruit to the EU; horticulture accounts for ~18% of Kenya's export earnings
  • Products: roses (60% of EU rose imports from Kenya), green beans, sugar snap peas, avocados; main areas: Naivasha (roses) and Central Highlands (vegetables)
  • Benefits: ~2 million people employed directly/indirectly; women ~75% of flower farm workers — significant female economic empowerment; consistent foreign exchange earnings; investment in cold storage, roads, and Nairobi airport freight capacity
  • Environmental challenges: Lake Naivasha water extraction lowering lake levels; pesticide runoff damaging lake ecology and wildlife; pressure on limited freshwater resources
  • Social challenges: low wages (~$4–6/day); seasonal employment insecurity; some reports of poor conditions and pesticide exposure
  • Fairtrade: many farms Fairtrade certified — premium payments fund community schools and health clinics; worker rights standards enforced

Topic 10 · Natural Resources & Sustainability

10.1–10.3▼

10.1 Types of Resources

  • Non-renewable — finite stock, cannot be replenished on human timescales: coal, oil, natural gas, metal ores
  • Renewable — replenished naturally and indefinitely: solar, wind, tidal, biomass, HEP (as long as rainfall continues)
  • Sustainable use — meeting current needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet theirs (Brundtland Commission, 1987)

Energy mix is shaped by: natural resource availability, level of economic development, environmental policy, energy security, and international agreements. Global trend toward renewables — but coal still provides ~36% of global electricity (2022).

10.2–10.3 Resource Management Case Studies

Case Study · Sustainable Land Management

Ethiopia — FMNR (Farmer Managed Natural Regeneration)

  • Context: The Sahel belt of Ethiopia suffered severe land degradation through deforestation, overgrazing, and intensive farming. Desertification and soil erosion threatened food security for millions.
  • What is FMNR? Farmers systematically protect and manage regrowth from existing root systems and stumps — no purchased seeds or saplings needed. Selective pruning of existing root networks allows natural regeneration.
  • Scale: adopted across millions of hectares; Tigray region alone saw ~900,000 ha restored over two decades
  • Outcomes: crop yields increased 25–45% in restored areas; more firewood and fodder; improved water infiltration; wells and springs returning; carbon sequestration recognised internationally
  • Why it works: no capital cost; uses indigenous farming knowledge; results visible within 2–5 years; spreads farmer-to-farmer without government intervention
  • Evaluation: widely regarded as one of the most cost-effective land restoration methods globally; championed by World Vision and the UN for Sahel-wide restoration (Great Green Wall initiative)
[PHOTO: FMNR success in Ethiopia — restored vegetation/tree cover on formerly degraded Sahel land, or thriving crops under regenerated trees]
Case Study · Renewable Energy — Solar CSP

Morocco Noor Ouarzazate Solar Complex

  • Edge of the Sahara; 2,900+ sunshine hours/year; world's largest Concentrated Solar Power (CSP) complex
  • Scale: Noor I (160 MW) + Noor II (200 MW) + Noor III (150 MW) + Noor PV (70 MW) = combined ~580 MW
  • CSP vs PV: CSP parabolic mirrors concentrate sunlight → heat molten salt to 500°C → salt stores heat → electricity generated for up to 7 hours after sunset. Thermal storage is CSP's key advantage over standard photovoltaic panels.
  • Benefits: reduces Morocco's fossil fuel import dependency (Morocco imports ~97% of its energy); contributes to 52% renewable electricity by 2030 target; powers 1.1 million homes; avoids 760,000 tonnes CO₂/year; ~2,000 construction jobs; solar technician training programmes
  • Challenges: high cost (~US$2.4 billion; funded by World Bank, AfDB, EU); large land footprint (~30 km²); mirrors need regular water cleaning — ironic in an arid region; local community concerns about land use and dust
[PHOTO: Morocco Noor Solar Complex — vast array of parabolic trough mirrors or the Noor III central power tower in the Sahara]
Case Study · Large-Scale HEP

Three Gorges Dam, Yangtze River, China

  • World's largest hydroelectric plant; completed 2006; dam 2,335m long, 185m high; reservoir stretches 600 km upstream; capacity 22,500 MW
  • Benefits: generates ~100 TWh/year (~6% of China's electricity; replaces ~50 million tonnes of coal/year); reservoir stores Yangtze floodwater; ocean-going vessels can now reach Chongqing 2,000 km inland
  • Costs:
    • Displacement: 1.24 million people relocated; resettlement often inferior; corruption in compensation payments documented
    • Cultural loss: hundreds of towns, archaeological sites, and cultural heritage submerged
    • Ecology: sediment trapped → downstream erosion; Yangtze River Dolphin (baiji) declared extinct 2006; Chinese sturgeon population collapsed; wetland habitat loss for migratory birds
    • Geological risk: reservoir pressure increased local earthquake activity; ongoing landslides on reservoir banks
  • Evaluation: delivers real energy and flood benefits — but at enormous human and ecological cost. Illustrates the difficult trade-offs in large-scale resource development: those bearing the costs (displaced communities, wildlife) differ from those receiving the benefits (urban electricity consumers, industry, flood-protected towns).
